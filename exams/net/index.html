UGC-NET / JRF English Literature

The full Paper 2 syllabus — ages, movements, criticism, theory and world writing — with the pattern, the marking, and a way through it.

What the examination is

The UGC National Eligibility Test is conducted by the National Testing Agency to determine eligibility for Assistant Professor and for the Junior Research Fellowship. English is subject code 30, and it is one of the most heavily attempted subjects in the humanities. The test is held in Computer-Based Test mode, and both papers are taken in a single uninterrupted sitting.

You need a Master's degree in the subject with at least 55 per cent marks — 50 per cent for candidates in reserved categories — to be eligible. Final-year postgraduate students may apply, subject to the conditions in the notification for that cycle.

Pattern and marking

  • All questions are objective multiple-choice. Each correct answer carries two marks.
  • There is no negative marking. Leaving a question blank gains you nothing, so attempt every one of the 150.
  • Total duration is three hours for both papers together. You may move between Paper 1 and Paper 2 freely within that time.
  • Qualification is decided on the combined aggregate of both papers, not on each paper separately. A strong Paper 1 genuinely lifts your total.

Pattern details change from cycle to cycle. Always confirm the number of questions, the marking scheme and the eligibility clauses against the official NTA notification for the session you are applying to.

The ten units of Paper 2

The English syllabus is divided into ten units. Units I to IV cover the literature itself and account for the largest share of questions; Units VIII and IX carry criticism and theory, which is where most candidates lose marks; Units V, VI, VII and X are smaller but very scoring, because the material is finite and rarely asked in a tricky way.

I

Drama

Greek and Roman drama, English drama from the miracle and morality plays through Marlowe, Shakespeare, Jonson and Webster, Restoration comedy, the eighteenth and nineteenth-century stage, Ibsen and Shaw, and modern and post-war drama including Beckett, Pinter, Osborne and Indian English playwrights such as Karnad and Tendulkar.

II

Poetry

Chaucer and the medieval poets, the sonnet tradition, Spenser, the Metaphysicals, Milton, the Augustans, the Romantics, the Victorians, the Modernists, the Movement poets, and Indian, American and postcolonial poetry in English.

III

Fiction, short story

The rise of the novel in the eighteenth century, the nineteenth-century realist tradition, the Victorian and modernist novel, stream of consciousness, the postmodern and postcolonial novel, the Indian English novel, and the short story as a distinct form.

IV

Non-Fictional Prose

Essays, biography and autobiography, travel writing, letters, journals and polemical prose — Bacon, Addison and Steele, Lamb, Hazlitt, Ruskin, Arnold, Orwell, Woolf's essays, and modern non-fiction.

V

Language: Basic concepts, theories and pedagogy. English in Use

Phonetics and phonology, morphology, syntax and semantics, sociolinguistics, varieties of English, error analysis, and approaches to English language teaching. Also practical English usage — this unit is often underestimated and answers here are quick to score.

VI

English in India: history, evolution and futures

Macaulay's Minute and colonial education policy, the growth of English studies in India, Indian English as a variety, translation, the bhasha–English relationship, and current debates about the language's place in Indian education.

VII

Cultural Studies

The Birmingham school, ideology and hegemony, popular culture, media, subculture, the culture industry, gender and identity, diaspora and globalisation — Williams, Hoggart, Hall, Gramsci, Bourdieu and after.

VIII

Literary Criticism

Classical criticism from Plato and Aristotle through Horace and Longinus, the Renaissance and Neoclassical critics, Dryden, Johnson, Wordsworth's Preface, Coleridge, Arnold, Eliot's essays, I. A. Richards, Leavis and the New Critics.

IX

Literary Theory post World War II

Structuralism and semiotics, poststructuralism and deconstruction, psychoanalysis, Marxist criticism, feminism, reader-response theory, New Historicism, postcolonialism, queer theory and ecocriticism — Saussure, Barthes, Derrida, Foucault, Lacan, Said, Spivak, Bhabha, Butler and others.

X

Research Methods and Materials in English

Research design, the thesis and dissertation, citation and documentation systems including MLA style, plagiarism and academic ethics, bibliography and reference tools, and the use of archives and digital resources.

How to work through it

Ten units in one year sounds impossible until you break the syllabus by type of effort rather than by unit number. Literature units need reading time and cannot be rushed. Theory needs repeated short revisions rather than one long study session. Language, English in India and Research Methods are essentially finite lists that can be learned in a few weeks and then simply maintained.

  1. Start with chronology, not with Unit I. Read the history of English literature age by age first — Old English through to the present. Once the timeline is in your head, every drama, poetry and fiction question has somewhere to attach itself.
  2. Take criticism and theory early. Units VIII and IX are the units students postpone and then never reach. Begin them in the first two months, in small doses, and keep returning.
  3. Clear the finite units quickly. Units V, VI and X are limited in scope. Finishing them early converts a vague syllabus into a manageable one and improves your aggregate immediately.
  4. Work previous-year papers as diagnosis, not as practice. After each paper, sort your wrong answers into three piles: did not know the fact, knew it but misread the question, and knew it but ran out of confidence. Each pile needs a different remedy.
  5. Revise in cycles. A unit studied once is a unit forgotten. Plan three passes over the whole syllabus, each shorter than the last, with the final pass being only your own condensed points.
