About AK Explained

A professor's attempt to put the whole of English literature on one organised, free shelf — for students preparing from BA to UGC-NET.

Who is behind AK Explained

My name is Afsar Khan. I teach English Literature, I hold a Ph.D. in the subject, and I have spent my working life in a classroom explaining the same texts to students who arrive with very different levels of preparation. Some of them have read Shakespeare in school; many have not read a full play in English before they reach the university gate. Both kinds of students sit for the same examination paper.

AK Explained began as a YouTube channel for exactly that second group. The idea was simple and slightly stubborn: if a student can follow a story in a Hindi film, that student can follow Doctor Faustus. What usually stands in the way is not intelligence but language, unfamiliar names, and study material written for people who already know the subject. So the videos explain literature in the language my students actually think in, and they do it without cutting the difficulty of the ideas themselves.

This website is the written half of the same work. Videos are good for understanding something the first time. They are poor for revision three days before an examination. Notes, syllabus maps, quizzes and reference pages are better for that, and this is where they live.

What this site is for

Three things, and deliberately not more than three.

  1. One organised shelf. English literature is taught in fragments — a poem here, a movement there, a theorist mentioned once and never explained. This site arranges the subject the way it actually developed, from Old English to the present, so that a student can see where any single text belongs.
  2. Material written for the examination in front of you. A note that helps in a BA semester paper is not the same note that helps in UGC-NET Paper 2. Each page states which examination it is written for, and stays inside that scope.
  3. No cost, no account, no catch. Everything here is free to read. There is no login wall, no paid tier, no course to buy at the end of a page. If a student with a low-end phone and an unreliable data pack can open a page and study from it, the page has done its job.

How the material is written

Two rules govern everything published here. The first is that nothing is invented. Dates, authorship, publication history, critical terms and the names of critics are taken from standard reference works — Andrew Sanders' Short Oxford History of English Literature, the Oxford Companion to English Literature, and M. H. Abrams' Glossary of Literary Terms — and where a fact is genuinely disputed among scholars, the page says so rather than choosing a tidy answer.

The second rule is that explanation comes before terminology. A student who understands what Eliot was doing in The Waste Land will remember the phrase "objective correlative" easily. A student who has memorised the phrase without the poem will lose it within a week. So the sequence on every page is the same: the text first, then the idea, then the technical name, then the points that examiners actually ask about.

Notes are written in Hinglish where that helps comprehension, and in English where precision matters more — literary theory, criticism and definitions of terms are usually in English, because the examination will use those exact words.
